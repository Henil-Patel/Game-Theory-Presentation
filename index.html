Henil Patel

Brief History

(1944) John Von Neumann & Oskar Morgenstern

Theory of Games and Economic Behavior published

...your outcomes are not only determined by your choices but also the choices of others

...much like in an economy, outcomes are based on the aggregate of decisions made by firms, consumers and governments

Revolutionized the analysis of decision making

(1950) John Nash

"Equilibrium Points in n-Person Games" published

Proved there exists a mixed strategy equilibrium in n-Person games (more on this later)

Motivation

"A game is a situation in which each participant's gain or loss is determined not only by their own actions but also by the actions of others."

In a sealed-bid auction, how much should a bidder bid given their private valuation of the item and their expected valuation by other bidders? - Auction Theory

If there are two firms (one price-setting and another price-taking), how much should each firm produce in response to the other? - Stackelberg Competition

How should countries achieve a common goal of reducing carbon emissions while balancing their own economic interest? - Tragedy of the Commons

How effective is the threat of retaliation to deter further warfare? - Deterrence Theory

Notation

$p \in P$ means $p$ is an element of $P$

$\sum_{i \in I} a_i$ means sum $a_i$ for all values of $i \in I$

$f: \R_1 \times ... \times \R_n \rightarrow \R$ means $f$ is a multi-variable function which maps a Cartesian product of Real Numbers (domain) to a Real Number (image)

$Pr: T \rightarrow [0,1]$ means a function which assigns probability to an event $t \in T$

$Pr(E | H)$ means the probability of event $E$ "given" event $H$

Model

$P$ - set of players $i = 1...n$

$S_i$ - set of strategies $s_i$ open to player $i$

$u: S_i \times S_{i'} \rightarrow \R$ - payoff function for player $i$ choosing strategy $s_i$ given strategies chosen by $i'$ players

Prisoner's Dilemma

Payoff Matrix

Two criminals are caught committing a crime...

Their strategies are $S = \{silent, defect\}$ and both are aware of each other's strategies.

Payoffs are defined in years of freedom from prison sentence (3 means 3 years of freedom earned)

Regardless of what player 2 does, player 1 is better off defecting! (By symmetry, player 2 will do the same)

Takeaway? Rational players acting in self-interest can lead to socially inefficient outcomes! 🤯

Best Response

Best Response: $s_i$ is a best response for player $i$ to rivals' strategies $s_{-i}$ if:

$u_i(s_i, s_{-i}) \geq u_i(s_{i'}, s_{-i})$ , $\forall s_{i'} \in S_i$

Denoted as $s_i \in BR(s_{-i})$

...if payoff from playing $s_i$ is at least as good as payoff from playing any $s_i'$, then $s_i$ is a best response (given other players have played $s_{-i}$)

Note the comparison is a weak inequality - this is to accomodate multiple best responses

Nash Equilibrium

Nash Equilibrium: A Nash equilibrium is a strategy profile $(s_1^*,..,s_n^*)$ such that for each player $i = 1,..,n$, $s_i^*$ is the best response to the other players' equilibrium strategies $s_{-i}^*$ i.e, $s_i^* \in BR(s_{-i}^*)$

For example

Consider $i = 1,2$, then $s^*_1, s^*_2$ form a NE if

$u_1(s^*_1, s^*_2) \geq u_1(s_1, s^*_2)$, $\forall s_1 \in S_1$ AND

$u_2(s^*_2, s^*_1) \geq u_2(s_2, s^*_1)$, $\forall s_2 \in S_2$

...players have no incentive to deviate their strategy even if they know the other players' strategy

Widely used result in economics, politics, philosophy, evolutionary biology and computer science

Mixed Strategies

Let set of M actions available to player $i$ be defined as $A_i = \{a^1_i, .., a^M_i\}$

A mixed strategy $s_i = \{\sigma^1_i,..,\sigma^M_i\}$ where $\sigma_i^m$ indicates the probability that $i$ plays $a^m_i$

Note that the sum of probabilities $\sigma^1_i+..+\sigma^M_i = 1$

For example

In case of prisoner's dilemma, a strategy could be $s_i = \{1/3, 2/3\}$

where $1/3$ is the probabiliy that player defects, $2/3$ is probability that the player remains silent

Mixed Strategy Nash Equilibrium

Recall the coordination game:

Let player $1$ play $s_1 = (x, 1-x)$

Let player $2$ play $s_2 = (y, 1-y)$

Where player $1$ plays movie with probability $x$ and player $2$ plays movie with probability $y$

Where action set $A = \{movie, concert\}$ and $a_i, a_j \in A$ for $i,j = 1,2$

Compute the expected payoff:

$U_1((x,1-x), (y, 1-y)) = \sum\limits_{i=1,2} \sigma_i (\sum\limits_{j=1,2} \sigma_j \cdot U_1(a_i, a_j)) = 1 - x - y + 3xy$

$U_2((y,1-y), (x, 1-x)) = \sum\limits_{j=1,2} \sigma_j (\sum\limits_{i=1,2} \sigma_i \cdot U_2(a_i, a_j)) = 2 - 2x - 2y + 3xy$

Mixed Strategy Nash Equilibrium

Recall

$U_1((x,1-x), (y, 1-y)) = 1 - x - y + 3xy$

$U_2((y,1-y), (x, 1-x))= 2 - 2x - 2y + 3xy$

Maximizing payoff (First order condition):

$\frac{dU_1}{dx} = -1 + 3y = 0 \implies y^* = 1/3$

If $y^* < 1/3$, player $1$ plays $x^*=0$, and if $y^* > 1/3$, player $1$ plays $x^*=1$ else they are indifferent.

$\frac{dU_2}{dy} = -2 + 3x = 0 \implies x^* = 2/3$

If $x^* < 2/3$, player $2$ plays $y^*=0$, and if $x^* > 2/3$, player $2$ plays $y^*=1$ else they are indifferent.

A mixed equilibrium is revealed at $x^* = 2/3, y^* = 1/3$

Recall pure strategy equilibria at $x^* = 1, y^* = 1$ (movie, movie) and $x^* = 0, y^* = 0$ (concert, concert)

Sequential Games

Games so far have been simultaneous

Consider the coordination game but sequential - player 1 goes first, player 2 goes second

Player 1 has pure strategy set $S_1 = \{movie, concert\}$

Player 2 however...$S_2=$

$(movie | movie, movie | concert)$ - always go to $movie$

$(movie | movie, concert | concert)$ - copy cat

$(movie | concert, concert | movie)$ - do the opposite

$(concert | movie, concert | concert)$ - always go to $concert$

Sequential Games

Recall for player 1 $movie \succsim concert$, and player 2 $concert \succsim movie$

Sequential Games

The following equilibria are observed:

(1) Player $1$ plays $movie$, Player $2$ plays $(movie | movie, movie | concert)$

(2) Player $1$ plays $movie$, Player $2$ plays $(movie | movie, concert | concert)$

(3) Player $1$ plays $concert$, Player $2$ plays $(concert | movie, concert | concert)$ - empty threat

Player $2$ threatens to play $concert$ regardless of player $1$'s choice - to coerce player $1$ to play $concert$

Since player $1$ goes first, this nullifies the threat as they can always shift to $movie$
